In Victorian times, when someone died, the family often called a photographer for one final portrait. But these weren't sad photos of a body in a coffin. Instead, the dead were dressed, posed in chairs, and propped up to look alive. Sometimes their eyes were painted open, or rosy cheeks were added later. A mother might cradle her dead child as if it were merely sleeping. And often the living relatives blurred because they moved, while the corpse stayed perfectly still. That stillness is how you can tell who had already died.
