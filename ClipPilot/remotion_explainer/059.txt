By 1858, the River Thames had become an open sewer, swallowing the waste of millions of people. Then a brutal heatwave hit, baking the riverbanks and cooking the filth into a stench so vile it earned a name: the Great Stink. The smell crept into Parliament itself, and lawmakers gagged behind curtains soaked in chemicals to mask it. They nearly fled the building entirely. But the unbearable reek finally forced them to act, funding a vast network of sewers. So it took a smell, not a disease, to finally clean up the city.
